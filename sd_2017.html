Organization

This year we'll have 11 classes and 3 tutorial sessions.

The classes will be every Tuesday and Thursday, except for Thursday March 23rd, when you'll enjoy a tutorial session. The other two tutorials are scheduled for Friday April 7th and Friday April 28th.

Following each of the first two tutorials you will receive a set of homework problems, intended to let you check your preparation. If you return the homework a week later (detailed instructions will follow) it will be corrected and returned to you.
I think that you should do and submit the homework, but it's strictly optional.

The slides I use will be posted here after each class, as well as solved problems and other classroom materials [1], but please understand that the materials (slides etc) that I post here are not a substitute for a real textbook (see below)
If you want to have a look at the slides before a class, last year's slides are a click away.

[1] Other classroom materials means mostly small computer programs, written in the Python programming language rather than in other languages with wich you might be most familiar. OTOH, in the tutorials we will use the programming languages you're possibly most familiar with.

Exam

The exam is based on a classroom written test and an oral test.

The written test is mostly an admission test for the oral exam, in other words your final mark will be based mainly on the oral exam.

Recommended text books

  • Anil K.Chopra, Dynamics of Structures (Theory and Applications to Earthquake Engineering), 4th ed.
  • Ray W. Clough, Joseph Penzien, Dynamics of Structures.
    This classic text is solely sold by the software house linked above.
    You can find an used copy with a bit of luck.

The course is mostly inspired by Clough and Penzien's book, but for many topics it follows Chopra's approach and examples. Should you prefer to buy a single book, my advice is "Buy Chopra's".

Note that copies of the 2nd and 3rd editions of Chopra's book (now at the 4th edition) are widely available at lower prices and these older editions are perfectly appropriate for my course. Note also that a fair number of copies of Chopra's book (3rd ed.) are available from the Campus Library in Lecco.

Homeworks

The homework problems sometimes require a little more than paper, pencil and a hand-held calculator, e.g. you have to plot a time series or perform some matrix algebra, but every half-decent spreadsheet (say Excel or Calc) can do these things.
If you are able to program a computer, there are programming languages tailored for the particular needs of an engineer, e.g. Mathematica, Matlab or Matlab's free clone Octave.
I'd like to mention that you can download from a Politecnico site Excel, Mathematica, Matlab and many other applications and development tools.

Imho a pleasant alternative to proprietary stuff is the Jupyter notebook (e.g., here it is the solution of a recently assigned problem).
In my classes I will use (and later distribute) a few notebooks to demonstrate simple programmatic solutions to complicated problems.
